From doing the busywork to deleting it

Laureen spent 10 years in medical billing — one of the most repetitive, deadline-driven jobs in business. That experience is exactly why her automations work in the real world.

Medical billing is relentless. Claims, follow-ups, denials, appeals, status checks — thousands of tiny, rules-based tasks that have to be done correctly and on time, every single day. Laureen did that work by hand for a decade. She knows what it feels like to lose a whole afternoon to a process a computer should be running.

So she started building computers to run it. Using AI models, Make.com, and Cloudflare Workers, she built her first agents to handle the repetitive parts of her own work — and then kept going.

"I don't automate from a whiteboard. I automate from ten years of being the person buried in the work."

Twenty agents, built while working full time

Laureen built more than 20 AI agents that run live operations — while holding a full-time job and raising her family as a single mom.

The agents aren't demos. They handle outreach, classify replies, follow up with leads, draft reports, run weekly business reviews, and watch for problems — the kind of end-to-end work most businesses still pay people to do manually.

She built them in the early mornings and late nights, one working system at a time. That's the standard she brings to client work: every engagement has to end with something that actually runs, not a strategy deck.

Building in public

Laureen shares her process openly on TikTok at @LearningAIwithLaureen, where she shows real people how AI agents and automation actually work.

The goal isn't hype. It's to make AI automation understandable for business owners who've been told it's either magic or a threat — when really it's just a tool that takes the boring work off your plate.

What Nicholson AI Group believes

Three principles shape every project: ship something that works, keep it in plain English, and never automate a process you don't understand first.

Every engagement ends with automation you can actually use. Strategy is part of the job, but it's never the whole deliverable.

Plain English, always

You'll never be buried in jargon. If you can describe the task, you can understand how it gets automated.

Understand, then automate
